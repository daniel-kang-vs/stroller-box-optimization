01 · Business Context & Stakes

The decision leadership needed to make

Fewer box variants — without a material jump in shipping cost

One box per product minimizes billable weight but floods the warehouse with packaging inventory, pick complexity, and replenishment overhead. Leadership wanted a smaller catalog — and a clear answer to how much shipping cost that standardization would actually cost.

Operational pressure

Seventeen unique boxes meant more slots, more stockouts risk, and higher handling cost. Ops needed a shared catalog that still covered every SKU.

Carrier pricing reality

Billable weight is the larger of actual weight and dimensional weight (L×W×H ÷ 250). Oversized shared boxes quietly inflate landed cost.

The tradeoff question

What is the smallest packaging set that keeps shipping cost close to a perfect-fit baseline — and where do the expensive outliers concentrate?

02 · Data Pipeline

From product specs to decision-ready inputs

Python workflows that structure constraints before optimization

The analysis starts with measurable product and packaging data — not a blank math model. Dimensions, weights, and fit rules become candidate assignments that can be scored on billable weight.

Product dimensions

2

3

Candidate boxes

Build a feasible set of 43 box types; validate fit with 0.5″ padding so assignments are warehouse-realistic.

4

Cost matrix

Score every product–box pair on billable weight, ready for the integer program to choose catalog and assignments.

03 · Methodology

Integer programming as a business tradeoff

Minimize billable shipping weight subject to a hard SKU cap

The model chooses which box types stay in the warehouse and which box each product uses. Framing it as a tradeoff — not a pure math exercise — makes the 7-box constraint the business lever: how much complexity can we cut before landed cost moves too far?

01 · Sets

Products and Candidate Boxes

Evaluate every product–box pair, then select a small catalog that still covers all products.

\[ \begin{aligned} I &= \{\text{candidate boxes}\} \\ J &= \{\text{stroller products}\} \end{aligned} \]
02 · Decision Variables

Assignment + Box Selection

\(x_{ij}\) assigns product \(j\) to box \(i\). \(y_i\) turns a box type on or off in the warehouse catalog.

\[ \begin{aligned} x_{ij} &\in \{0,1\} \quad \text{product } j \text{ uses box } i \\ y_i &\in \{0,1\} \quad \text{box } i \text{ is stocked} \end{aligned} \]
03 · Objective Function

Minimize Total Billable Weight

Assignment cost equals what the carrier charges: the larger of actual product weight and box dimensional weight.

\[ \begin{aligned} \min_{x,y} \quad & \sum_{i \in I}\sum_{j \in J} c_{ij}x_{ij} \\ c_{ij} = & \max\left(w_j,\frac{L_i W_i H_i}{250}\right) \end{aligned} \]
04 · Constraints

One Assignment, Seven Boxes, Feasible Fit

Each product gets one box, products can only use active types, and the active catalog is capped at seven — the operational complexity lever.

\[ \begin{aligned} \sum_{i \in I} x_{ij} &= 1 && \forall j \in J \\ x_{ij} &\le y_i && \forall i \in I,\; j \in J \\ \sum_{i \in I} y_i &\le 7 \\ x_{ij} &= 0 && \text{if product } j \text{ does not fit in box } i \end{aligned} \]

05 · Business Insights & Quantified Impact

59% fewer packaging SKUs for a 6.5% cost penalty

The recommendation leadership can act on

Compared with a perfect-fit baseline (one ideal box per product), the optimized 7-box system adds only 6.5% billable weight — a small shipping-cost premium for cutting packaging SKUs by more than half and giving ops a clear complexity–cost frontier.

Executive takeaway

Standardize on 7 shared box types. Accept a 6.5% shipping-weight penalty to eliminate 59% of packaging SKUs. Most of the penalty concentrates in two outliers (Vista V2, Fox 5) — a focused exception policy can shrink the residual further.
